# Abstract {.unnumbered}

Operating an isolated photovoltaic microgrid means making decisions with little information and under uncertainty: one must anticipate how much energy will be generated and consumed over the coming hours, estimate how long the storage system can sustain demand, and detect performance losses—such as panel soiling—in time. Direct supervision and conventional monitoring systems record what has already happened, but they do not anticipate what is about to happen, so planning battery use and maintaining the installation rely largely on the operator's experience. This Diploma Thesis addresses that gap with the development of a web-based digital twin for isolated photovoltaic microgrids, taking the microgrid of the Technological University of Havana "José Antonio Echeverría" (CUJAE) as a case study. The system monitors the microgrid in real time, forecasts generation and consumption, simulates storage autonomy, and diagnoses the cleanliness of the panels; to this end it integrates four artificial-intelligence models. The solution was designed so that every site-specific parameter resides in data rather than in code, allowing it to be applied to other microgrids without reprogramming. In the experimental analysis, carried out with real data from Havana and public datasets, solar generation forecasting reached a coefficient of determination of 0.90 and the panel-cleanliness classifier an accuracy of 84 %; the system's behaviour was verified through test cases and a suite of automated tests, and it was deployed on the CUJAE microgrid as the application scenario. The results show that the digital twin provides useful and sufficiently accurate support for the operation, analysis, and maintenance of isolated photovoltaic microgrids, and that it is transferable to other sites.

**Keywords:** digital twin, isolated photovoltaic microgrid, solar forecasting, machine learning, energy autonomy.
